A Close Reading of Knaflic's Chapter 6

14 September 2021 | Words: 543 | Theory

Throughout chapters one to five of Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic’s 2015 book, Storytelling with Data (Knaflic, 2015), we are shown multiple techniques to effectively visualize data – with the goal of portraying a particular narrative and making the data more understandable. In chapter six however, she takes this information and applies it to model examples – highlighting key visualization techniques across different line, and bar, graphs. As such, in this blog post I will be doing a close reading of chapter six, focusing on the key visualization techniques that Knaflic highlights, referring to one of the model graphs in particular to illustrate her points.

Throughout all of the model graphs that Knaflic dissects, she points to the importance of leveraging visual affordances to aid the audience in understanding visualizations. She notes that preattentive attributes of size, colour, and position should be used to both critically: emphasize important information through brighter, more bold, elements; and de-emphasize unimportant information through dull, smaller, elements. This understanding can then be used to help focus the audience’s attention, creating good visual hierarchies to direct the audiences eyes around the visualization. An example of this can be seen in figure 1 below, which is a model visualization that Knaflic dissects, where the audience’s attention is drawn to the “Progress to date” elements because of their vibrant, thicker, appearance. Thus by highlighting important information, eliminating distractions, and establishing a good visual hierarchy, better information processing can be achieved.

Thereafter, Knaflic points to the importance of making visualizations accessible to all – highlighting how they must not be overly complicated, while still providing any important information without confusion. One way to do this is through the use of text – particularly by using clear labels, titles, and annotations only where necessary. For example, we can see how this knowledge has been applied in figure 1 above, where everything is appropriately labelled and annotated for easy understanding, with the lines even labelled directly to allow for better readability. Not only that, but since the purpose of the graph is to show a general trend, the x-axis has also been simplified to only show 5 day intervals.

Lastly, Knaflic highlights the importance of having good aesthetic features in order to improve the readability of the visualization. One of the main points she highlights here is that elements must be properly position and aligned – creating a sense of cohesion between them, showing the audience how to read the visualization. Furthermore, by using things such as the gestalt principle of proximity, the relation between elements is instantly clear – all while being more aesthetically pleasing. These ideas can be seen in figure 1 above, where all the axes elements are aligned correctly, and the information related to “Progress to date” is both grouped together and right-aligned.

In conclusion, although the chapter could have conveyed all the main points through one graph, the use of multiple was done purposefully to show how these key visualization techniques can be used in various ways. Although, it is also important to note that although these techniques are extremely useful, the list is not exhaustive and as such it is possible to use differing techniques – as long as all decisions are made mindfully and intentional.

References
