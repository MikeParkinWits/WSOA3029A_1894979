Homepage

While the user interface (UI), and experience (UX), design of websites are often overlooked, they are extremely important – as a bad UI/UX design will result in an unusable website, with an unnecessary loss of potential visitors. As such, in this blog post I will be critically analyzing the effectiveness of the UI and UX design of the Apple.com homepage, with the aid of various theorists to substantiate my analysis.

Blog Selection Page

Firstly, we can start by analyzing the UX of the website’s homepage – which revolves around looking at the experience the user has when visiting the website to achieve a specific task. However, in order to analyse this, we must first understand what Apple does. Apple is an American consumer technology company that specializes in selling products and services with fluid, and polished, experiences. As such, there are two potential goals that a user would want to achieve on their website: to learn about, or buy, their products. With that in mind, we can then begin to analyse the websites homepage.

Blog Content Page

Upon landing on Apple.com’s homepage, we see that it aligns well to the users’ potential goals – as the first thing displayed is a splash-screen showing off one of their latest products, with buttons to either buy, or learn more about, it. This same concept is then repeated throughout the entire homepage, allowing quick access to the products that the user is most likely to be interested in. However, if the product the user wants is not on the homepage, they can then use the persistent navigation bar to easily view different product categories, or even search for the specific product. Not only that, but this navigation bar also uses the logo as a home button, presenting a good conceptual model for the user – which is the mental model of how we believe an object should work. This is important in UX design, as Don Norman says in his 1988 book The Design of Everyday Things, good conceptual model allows us to predict the effects of our actions (Norman, 1988: 13).

Furthermore, we can then look at the website’s UI – which is its visual design. As you can see in figure 1 below, the website is extremely minimalistic in its design. This minimalism allows for Apples colourful products to stand out, acting as the main focus point of the homepage. Not only that, but the website then uses contrasting colours to differentiate between these different products – creating a visually appealing design that is easily readable. However, this minimalism does not decrease the websites usability, as all important interactable objects are still clear – which can be seen through the contrasting bright blue hyperlinks, and the navigation bar items that change shades when hovered over. Although, while many see this minimalist design as awe inspiring, theorists such as Jenny Kidd, in their 2015 book Representation, would argue that all the minimalistic visuals do is exacerbate the myth present around Apple and their products (Kidd, 2015: 23 - 24).

In conclusion, we can then see that the design of Apple.com’s homepage presents a good UI and UX design in order to match their proposed brand image of a fluid experience, all while still meeting the users goals of finding, and buying, products. This good design is then furthered by following previously established conceptual models and good visual indicators for users.
