A Close Reading of 'Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics'

19 October 2021 | Words: 495 | Theory

Although data visualizations have traditionally been viewed as a highly scientific, and analytical, way of displaying data visually; with easier access to more powerful computers, and big data, this view has started to shift – allowing for the creation of the artistic data visualization. As such, in this blog post I aim to explore what an artistic data visualization is, by providing a close reading of Fernanda Viégas and Martin Wattenberg’s 2007 article Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics (Viégas and Wattenberg, 2007). Thereafter, I conclude by reflecting on the article and its significance.

Firstly, we must begin by defining what an artistic data visualization is. According to Viégas and Wattenberg, artistic data visualizations require two important factors. Firstly, the artwork must rely on actual datasets, as opposed to merely mimicking the appearance of a data visualization; and secondly, they must avoid using visual aesthetics as a defining characteristic of the artwork, instead relying on artistic intent as a driving force. Thus, the authors then use these two elements to devise a definition of artistic data visualizations, concluding that they are “visualizations of data done by artists with the intent of making art” (Viégas and Wattenberg, 2007: 2; emphasis added).

With the definition of an artistic data visualization outlined, Viégas and Wattenberg then use a series of examples (one of which can be seen in figure 1 below) to highlight some of the central qualities of these visualizations. By doing this, they then note that each artistic data visualization commits a different ‘sin’ of traditional data visualizations – such as in figure 1 below, where Jason Salavon purposefully adjusts his data, aligning images to better illustrate his point. From this, we can then see that in contrast to traditional data visualizations, which try to minimize personal bias, artistic data visualizations purposefully embody a forceful point-of-view. Thus, Viégas and Wattenberg argue that instead of trying to minimize the inherently bias point-of-view of the designer when creating traditional visualizations, they should embrace it – allowing for both dispassionate data visualizations, and more persuasive data visualizations, to be recognized equally from an analytical standpoint.

In conclusion, we can then see that in their article, Viégas and Wattenberg provide an extremely succinct definition for what an artistic data visualization is. Not only that, but using examples, they also argue that although artistic visualizations have their roots in traditional visualizations, the artistic data visualizations’ reliance on a perspective should be adopted in the creation of traditional visualizations. So, while I originally started this article thinking it would be purely about defining an artistic data visualization, I found the point of accepting inherent bias in traditional visualizations fascinating. However, I view this as slippery slope, as incorporating a point-of-view into traditional, analytical, data visualizations could result in malicious information being spread more easily. As such, I believe that they should not start considering perspectives, instead staying as unbiased as possible – allowing for a clear understanding that the information being viewed is factual.
