A Close Reading of Chapter 9 of 'Data Driven Storytelling'

02 November 2021 | Words: 549 | Theory

While ethics are an important field of research in media, they have often been overlooked when it comes to data visualizations. Thus, in this blog post I aim to provide a close reading of chapter 10 of the book Data-Driven Storytelling, where Nicholas Diakopoulos attempts to provide a framework for better understanding ethical decisions in data-driven stories (Diakopoulos, 2018). Afterward, I then reflect on the chapter and the ethical framework that it introduces, highlighting my thoughts on its usefulness.

In chapter 10, Diakopoulos introduces several ethical decisions that must be considered when creating data-driven stories, which he categorized into three different phases below:

Data Acquisition

  1. Provenance: The origin of the data – such as its current state, legitimacy, and author – must be considered, in order to evaluate whether the chosen dataset can used to create informative, and truthful, visualizations.
  2. Quantification: The way data is measured and quantified is extremely important, as it must be relevant and allow for the data-driven story to be as faithful to reality as possible.

Data Transformation

  1. Normalization: Data must be correctly, and appropriately, normalized; as the insight that a reader gains from the visualization comes from the designer’s choices in normalization.
  2. Aggregation: The aggregation of data must be consistent throughout the data visualization, otherwise the visualization risks being misunderstood and deceiving the end-user.
  3. Algorithmic Derivation: When manipulating data using more sophisticated algorithms, errors that occur must be mitigated, and made known otherwise – in order to not deceive the end-user.
  4. Filtering: Although filtering data may be necessary to highlight certain aspects of the data, it must be done with caution as to not cherry-pick data and potentially sway the audience’s view.
  5. Anonymization: The narrative of the story must not be told at the expense of the research participant, and as such any personal information must be anonymized where possible – helping to alleviate risks for the participant.

The Connection of Insight

  1. Visual Mapping and Representation: The way in which data is mapped and represented must not be distorted, allowing the end-user not to be persuaded by visual manipulations.
  2. Implied Relationships: The temporal and sequential relationship between data must always be considered, only conveying relevant information that is justifiable and factual.
  3. Context and Annotation: The annotations, and context, of the visualization must be clear, concise, and relevant – allowing the audience to come to a true and logical interpretation of the data.
  4. Interactivity: Although interactivity allows end-users to come to their own conclusion on the meaning of a visualization, designers must still carefully consider the default values for the interaction – as some users may only interact these values, resulting in assumptions being made based on this.

As a result, we can then see that while the elements that Diakopoulos outlines may seem obvious at first, and act as a starting point in understanding ethics in data visualizations; by providing a concise breakdown of each, we are then able to see just how important they are when creating a data-driven story. This is extremely important to me, as I believe that ethical considerations should always be made when dealing with any data. As such, I see the breakdown of these point as crucial, and will be utilizing them to both evaluate eternal data visualizations, on top of using them to make sure all my future data visualizations are ethically sound.
