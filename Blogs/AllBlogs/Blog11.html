An Analysis of an interactive data visualization from InformationIsBeautiful

12 October 2021 | Words: 546 | Theory

While interactivity is not a prerequisite for a good data visualization, when implemented correctly it can greatly enhance the visualization. As a result, in this blog post I will be analyzing an interactive data visualization, from informationisbeautiful.net, that visualizes the legitimacy of legal sport supplements that are commonly taken by athletes. To do this, I start by briefly looking at its form and narrative; before assessing the success of both the visualizations interactivity and use of semiotic devices.

Firstly, it is important to understand both the narrative being visualized, and its form. In this data visualization, David McCandless presents us with some of the most common forms of sport supplements taken by athletes, ranking them in order of effectiveness (McCandless, n.d.). However, while he could have displayed this information in an ordered list, he chose to utilize a bubble chart – where the commonly used supplements are represented by bigger bubbles, along an x-axis that goes from strong, to no, scientific evidence of effectiveness. From this, we can deduce that McCandless is trying to shed light on the effectiveness of different sport supplements.

With that in mind, we can start examining the success of this data visualizations interactivity. According to Dimara and Perin, in their 2020 paper What is Interaction for Data Visualization?, there are seven components that can be used, in combination with one another, to evaluate interaction for data visualizations: interplay, person, data interface, action, action-reaction, reaction perceived as such, and data-related intent (Dimara and Perin, 2020). To evaluate this in our visualization, we can use the example of an athlete searching for information about a specific supplement. Upon landing on the visualization, the person can instantly deduce information about a specific supplement – or even explore other, potentially more effective, supplements. If they then want to learn more about a supplement, they can easily hover over any bubble and the system reacts accordingly by expanding the bubble, presenting more information about the supplements effectiveness.

From this, we then see that this visualization allows for a level of interplay between a person and a data interface, facilitating a data-related intent of searching for information about a supplement. Not only that but when interacting with a bubble, an appropriate reaction is presented through the bubble expanding. Although, this reaction is not always perceived as intended, as its outcome is not always consistent – as some bubbles expand to show more detailed information, whereas others prompt must be clicked further for more information.

However, interactive visualizations are only useful when it is clear that they are interactive. To understand how this is achieved, we look to Jenny Kidd who, in their 2016 book Representation, states that meaning is made up of signs and codes (Kidd, 2016). Using this idea, we then see that McCandless choice of a bubble chart is extremely clever, as he utilizes the preconceived semiotic device of bubble charts – that they can be hovered over to reveal more information – to allow users to easily know that they are interactable.

In conclusion, while there may be points where the visualization does not provide the user with information as expected, it does present the user with good visual feedback and make use of good semiotic devices. Thus, I believe that it successfully utilizes interactivity to enhance the visualization, advancing the narrative that it is being told.
