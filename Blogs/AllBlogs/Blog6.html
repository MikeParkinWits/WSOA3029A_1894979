Initial Website Styleguide

10 September 2021 | Words: 543 | Website Development

Data visualization has a deep and rich history. One of the most prominent inventors in this field is William Playfair, who conceptualized some of today’s most common data visualization forms – including pie charts, bar graphs, and line graphs. As a result, I will be analyzing one of Playfair’s most popular data visualizations, one of the first recorded bar graphs. To do this, I first review the visualization and story it is trying to tell; and then analyze it, looking at its short comings and how it can be improved by applying modern data visualization concepts.

Firstly, I start by looking at the graph (as per figure 1 above), which displays three different pieces of information spanning ~250-years in the United Kingdom: the average weekly wage of mechanics on the line graph, the price of wheat on the bar graph, and the ruling monarch on the timeline. By placing all this information on one graph we can see that Playfair wanted to illustrate the relationship between a laborer’s weekly wages and the increased price of wheat in the UK. At the time, this type of data visualization, with different empirical data on one graph, was revolutionary. While this met Playfair’s idea that data must speak to the eyes (Playfair in Thompson, 2016: n.p.) to teach meaning, this meaning is lost in the age of modern data visualizations.

Therefore, we can start to closely analyse the visualization. In Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic’s 2015 book Storytelling With Data, we are introduced to the idea that all visual elements on a data visualization take up a certain amount of cognitive load, and as such only elements that increase understanding should be included to reduce ‘clutter’ (Knaflic, 2015: 71-73). As a result, it is crucial that the story behind the visualization is clear – in this case, looking at the price of wheat relative to labour wages over the years. Thus, we can then see Playfair’s data visualization has various elements that are ‘clutter’ in relation to the story being told – these include the doubling of the y-axis, the timeline of monarchs, and the centuries at the top. Furthermore, while Knaflic highlights the importance of using colour, it should only be used to aid the visualization, and the random use of pink and blue, highlighting the wage increase, overpowers the price of wheat and ultimately creates an unequal balance between the two.

Next, placing the graphs side-by-side leaves much of its meaning to interpretation. As Jenny Kidd says in their 2016 book Representation, the more abstract an image the more foregrounded its connotations (Kidd, 2016: 27). As there is no obvious correlation between the data in figure 1, other than being on the same page, one could easily misinterpret the meaning of the visualization. As such, in Friendly’s 2008 book section A Brief History of Data Visualizations (Friendly, 2008), he recommends a different graph all together – a graph that compares the ratio of wages to price over the years on one graph, alleviating confusion about its meaning. However, it is important to note that none of these ideas had been conceptualized at the time.

In conclusion, we can then see that although Playfair did manage to make important discoveries relating to data visualization, we have come a long way since his discoveries – establishing many standards, and new graph types, to improve data visualizations their stories.
