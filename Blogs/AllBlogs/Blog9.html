An Analysis of a visualization from Information Is Beautiful

25 September 2021 | Words: 547 | Website Development

Data visualizations are extremely useful, and when utilized correctly can teach valuable information. As such, in this blog post I will be analyzing a data visualization, from informationisbeautiuful.net, that portrays the 500 used common passwords in the world. To do this, I start by establishing the narrative that is trying to be told. Thereafter, I use this to analyze the data visualization – identifying elements that are utilized to help represent the visualizations narrative more effectively.

Firstly, we can start by looking at the narrative that is trying to be portrayed. In this data visualization by David McCandless, we are presented with 500 of the most used passwords (McCandless, n.d.). However, McCandless does not merely present these in an ordered table, nor utilize a more traditional form of data visualization such as a vertical bar graph. Instead, he chose to use a word cloud – a form of data visualization where the more common a word, the bigger it is, and vice versa. As such, we can conclude that McCandless it trying to show what the most common passwords are, in the hopes that people avoid using them.

With this, we can then begin analysing how successfully the data visualization portrays this narrative. To do this, I use Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic’s idea of preattentive attributes, which she notes, in her 2015 book Storytelling with Data, are an important tool in creating good data visualizations (Knaflic, 2015). According to Knaflic, preattentive attributes – such as size, colour, and position – help to both direct the audience, and create a good visual hierarchy (ibid.). Using this, we can then see that McCandless’ use of a word cloud was purposeful, as it makes use of the preattentive attribute of size to highlight the most common passwords – focusing the audiences gaze on them. Furthermore, this choice allows for good positionality, as the most prominent words are clustered around the middle; while the less prominent words are on the outskirts – resulting in a good visual hierarchy.

However, McCandless use of colour tells a different story. Breaking away from the narrative of the most used passwords, he uses colour to highlight different password categories – such as alphanumeric, names, animals, etc… While this may seem useful at first, it is actually extremely deceptive. When viewing the visualization, the audience sees that there are large amounts of red, making the assumption that the most common password category is alphanumeric; however, in a footnote he writes that only 13% of the most used passwords are alphanumeric, with 36% being names. This most likely occurs as the top most used, bigger, passwords might be alphanumeric, while majority of the other, smaller, passwords are mostly names. This then aligns with Jenny Kidd, who notes, in her 2016 book Representation, that the way information is represented is important, and can potentially alter the way we perceive things (Kidd, 2016). In this case, as we saw more red it was assumed that it was the majority, even though it was not.

As such, through the use of the preattentive attributes of size and position, McCandless is able to portray the intended narrative well – allowing the audience to easily understand which passwords must be avoided. However, the addition of password categories should be rethought as it is both misleading and strays from the main narrative.
