A Critical Analysis of my Website Design

02 September 2021 | Words: 550 | Theory

User Interface (UI) and User Experience (UX) design are two fundamental parts of design and must be carefully considered when designing a website. As such, in this blog post I will be critically analyzing the UI and UX design of my website, looking specifically at the homepage as it encompasses design decisions throughout the entire website. However, as my site is still under development, I will mainly be referring to my wireframes and style guide to illustrate my points.

Firstly, we can start by looking at the UX design of my website’s homepage in relation to the founder of the term’s two main requirements for a good UX design – Don Norman. In his seminal work The Design of Everyday Things, Norman states that a good UX design must always meet the needs of the user without any confusion (Norman, 1998: XIV). As such, when designing the UX for my website I made sure to question what the needs of a user visiting my website are. This led to two main goals for any user visiting my site: viewing data visualizations to learn about data breaches and leaks; and reading my blog posts. Thus, when the user first lands on my websites homepage I cater for this by providing two call-to-action buttons, asking whether the user wants to view the latest data visualizations, or the latest blog posts – scrolling down the homepage automatically to display either when clicked.

Next, Norman’s second requirement notes that a truly good UX goes beyond just giving the user what they say they want – in a simple, and accessible, way (Norman, 1998: XIV). As such, I also provided a navigation bar that sticks to the top of the screen, always providing access to every section of the website. On top of this, as Jenny Kidd states in their 2016 book Representation, signs and codes work to indicate meaning in texts… and to present certain things as logical and natural, and by extension other readings as illogical (Kidd, 2016: 22). As a result of this, I made a conscious effort to only use the most common signs when creating the navigation bar – such as keeping its placement on the top and using the website logo as a home button.

Thereafter, we can look at the UI design of my website’s homepage, which can be broken down into two components – starting with the design of elements. Throughout the website, I have chosen to keep a minimalistic design – utilizing flat objects with sharp corners, alongside a neutral colour scheme. This was done to both allow users to instantly identify objects while also allowing important information to standout by using contrasting colours. Not only that, but there were various other considerations to improve the websites readability – such as consistent lines and contrasting backgrounds to segment sections, buttons that animate when hovered over to show that they are interactable, and clear and simple fonts throughout the website. Secondly, we can then look at the layout of the design – using ideas outlined by Luis Francisco-Revilla and Jeff Crow in their paper Interpreting the Layout of Web Pages, noting how web pages can have modular layouts (Francisco-Revilla and Crow, 2009: 158). As such, I have chosen to place information modularly to allow for easier reading and less linear information intake.
