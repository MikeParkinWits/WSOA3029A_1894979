A Close Reading of 'What is Interaction for Data Visualization?'

05 October 2021 | Words: 600 | Theory

While most people are familiar with the term interaction, it becomes difficult to define in relation to visualizations. Thus, in this blog post I provide a close reading of Evanthia Dimara and Charles Perin’s 2020 text What is Interaction for Data Visualization?, wherein they compile various papers in order to provide a succinct definition of interaction for visualizations (Dimara and Perin, 2020). Thereafter, I briefly conclude by reflecting on the paper and the definition provided.

After outlining their research methodology, Dimara and Perin start by looking at the current understanding of interaction for visualization – observing that existing definitions are broad and elusive. However, from these they note several core concepts of interaction for visualizations (noted in bold), which can be compiled to create the following definition: a dialogue between a single human user and a visualization system – wherein the user performs an action that is intent-driven, and the interface returns a reaction within a certain time. Although, they see these concepts as lacking flexibility – with human and technological modalities overlooked, a polysemic vocabulary of terms, and no consensus for the importance of intent.

As such, to address this lack of flexibility, Dimara and Perin turn their attention to how interactions are viewed in Human-Computer Interactions (HCI) – stating that HCI can be used to extend concepts of interaction for visualizations in three ways. Firstly, the interaction in HCI is not always intent-driven, and instead must merely have a clear intent – whether driven by it or not. Secondly, while both views of interaction account for an entity interacting with the system, HCI allows for a broader spectrum of entities. In fact, the authors take this further, replacing the term user with person – allowing for entities such as animals, or AI, to be included. Thirdly, what makes a good interaction can be extended. While the term dialogue has been criticized as too limiting, only considering sequential human-to-human interfaces; by considering the seven core components of HCI – dialogue, transmission, control, tool use, optimal behavior, embodiment, experience – it can be replaced with interplay, which includes more interfaces.

However, there are also fundamental differences between visualization and HCI interaction, with visualizations relying more on data. As such, the authors broaden two additional concepts. Firstly, to be more inclusive, and less reliant on technology, they rephrased the visualization system as a data interface to emphasize the data over the system. Secondly, they note that while it is important that the reaction time of a system should be instant, it is more important that the reaction is perceived as such by the person.

As a result, by extending the definition of the visualization view of interaction, using HCI, Dimara and Perin note that the seven mandatory components of interaction for visualization are interplay, person, data interface, action, action-reaction, reaction perceived as such, and data-related intent. Thus, by compiling these, they find that “interaction for visualization is the interplay between a person and a data interface involving a data-related intent, at least one action from the person and an interface reaction that is perceived as such” (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 8).

With that in mind, we then see that although the paper is quite dense, it provides an extremely useful set of components for evaluating interactive data visualizations. Furthermore, by utilizing these components when creating data visualizations, interactivity can be more seamlessly integrated into the visualization, allowing it to enhance the narrative being told through the visualization. As such, going forward I will definitely be utilizing the seven components that Dimara and Perin highlight as a starting point for both creating, and evaluating the success of, interactive data visualizations.
