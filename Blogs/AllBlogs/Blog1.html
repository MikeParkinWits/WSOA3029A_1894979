A Close Reading of Overview of Data Visualization

17 August 2021 | Words: 568 | Theory

Data Visualization has long been an important tool for understanding data, and in the age of powerful, and precise, computing its usefulness has increased exponentially. As a result, in this blog post I will be providing a close reading of chapter 2 of Qi Li’s 2020 book Embodying Data, entitled “Overview of Data Visualization” (Li, 2020: 17–47). Thereafter, I conclude by briefly reflecting on the chapter and its insights.

Firstly, Li begins by exploring various authors definitions of data visualization to understand its purpose, landing on the idea that it is about congregating raw, unprocessed, data into a graphical, processed, form. This processed form then creates new information about a particular topic, highlighting the relationship between raw data and the information that comes from processing it. Furthermore, Li then goes on to explain how data visualization can be broken down into two major types: information visualization, which transforms abstract data into information; and scientific visualization, which transforms scientific, physically based, data into information.

Thereafter, the chapter goes on to explore various forms of data visualization – identifying the use case for each. The most common of these forms, and their ideal use cases, are:

Information Visualizations

  1. Tables: The most common form, consisting of several rows and coloumns of data with the goal of comparing the relationship between absolute data – specifically useful for displaying precise numerical values
  2. Charts: A form which portrays data in diagrams, tables, or graphs – usually used to represent quantitative data to portray size and dimension information
  3. Trees: A hierarchical structure that makes use of nesting to display larger datasets, with the goal of organizing, rationalizing, and illustrating information patterns
  4. Maps: A visual representation of the relationship between various data elements pertaining to a space
  5. Scatter Plots: Using a Cartesian plane to plot points of multivariable data containing x and y coordinates
  6. Diagrams: A form that is similar to a chart, but focuses on showing the flow between different data
  7. Graphs: While similar to diagrams and charts, graphs are mainly used to portray broad insight into data for an overview, as opposed to accurate accounts

Scientific Visualizations

  1. Simulations: The use of computers to simulate data pertaining to natural phenomena, allowing insight into the changes of these phenomena overtime
  2. Charts: Data visualizations that take the form of a signal, specifically for scientific data that occurs over a short time frame
  3. Trees: The most important scientific visualization, usually allowing multi-dimensional datasets to be portrayed on a flat, 2D, surface

However, Li explains that no matter what visualization techniques you use, there are common characteristics that should be kept throughout – as justified through research into the human perception process. These characteristics include readability, which dictates how easily the data visualization can be understood; recognizability, which is the ability to relate it to a previous knowledge base; and meaning, which looks at how well the visualization portrays itself.

In conclusion, although many presume the knowledge presented in Li’s text to be assumed and simple, the intricacies of what Li presents are far deeper. By fully understanding what data visualization is, its two different types, its most common forms, and their common characteristics; designers are then able to fully utilize it to best turn data into information. However, having this sort of understanding also shows how the use of clever data visualization can be used to skew someone into believing fake information. This ultimately shows the moral obligation that designers have when creating data visualizations.

References
