Top 10 Biggest Digital Data Breaches

Reflection

28 September 2021 | Words: 517 | Data Visualization

Creating a data visualization is an extremely involved process, and thus it is important that every aspect of its creation is carefully considered. As such, in this blog post I will be critically reflecting on the first data visualization I created for my website. To do this, I start by explaining both the reasons, and narrative, behind the type of data visualization chosen. Thereafter, I elaborate on this by looking more closely at the considerations made when creating my data visualization.

Firstly, we can start by looking at both the information I intended to portray through my data visualization, and the type of data visualization I chose to represent this information. As this is the first data visualization on my website, I knew that it would act as a starting point for both people visiting my site, and potential future data visualizations. As such, when brainstorming I landed on the simple idea of comparing the top 10 biggest digital data breaches in history – with the goal of showing the relative scale between each while also highlighting the biggest numbers of data lost. With this, I then began thinking of ways I could visualize this information – keeping in mind that this was my first time working with the D3.js library, and as a result it would be simple. After considering various types of visualizations, I decided that as I wanted to compare the size of different data breaches, the best option would be a horizontal bar-chart – as Qi Li states, in chapter 2 of her 2020 book Embodying Data, that charts are best used to portray size and dimension information (Li, 2020: 29 - 30). On top of this, while I did consider using a regular vertical bar-chart, I felt that a horizonal bar-chart would be better suited, as the data being displayed was categorical and could be ordered more effectively.

Although, deciding on the narrative, and visualization type, is only the first step of creating a data visualization – as such, we can start looking at the creation process behind the visualization itself. An important idea that Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic puts forward, in her 2015 book Storytelling with Data, is that data visualizations should only contain the most crucial information, as any additional information is clutter and adds to the readers cognitive load (Knaflic, 2015: 71 - 72). Thus, I made sure to only add the most important information to the chart – with elements such as the bars, company names, and a simplified x-axis. On top of this, I considered differentiating each company’s bar with their brand colour however, I found that this unnecessarily increased the reader’s cognitive load. Instead, I opted for a single red colour, alongside paler text and grid line colours, to help the data stand out in comparison to one another

To conclude, in this blog post I looked at the reasoning behind the chosen type of data visualization and the information I intended to tell show with it. Furthermore, I then elaborated on this by reflecting on the considerations made with the chart, looking at how the readers cognitive load was considered throughout.
