Reflection

01 December 2021 | Words: 610 | Data Visualization

While data visualizations have traditionally been known as an unbiased and objective way of portraying information; the introduction of data-driven artworks has marked a shift towards a more subjective and artistic data visualization. However, just because data-driven artworks are not governed by the same rules that data visualizations are does not mean they have no governing principles whatsoever. As such, in this blog post I aim to critically reflect on the interactive data-driven artwork that I created, using Fernanda Viegas and Martin Wattenberg’s 2007 text Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics (2007) as a benchmark to do so.

a full understanding of what a data-driven artwork is and how it differs from a traditional data visualization. As noted in the introduction, unlike their counterpart, data-driven artworks encourage the perspective of the creator to be at the forefront of the visualization. To achieve this, Viégas and Wattenberg note that artists frequently subvert predefined constructs of traditional data visualizations – as they have traditionally been used to minimize perspective. Although, while data-driven art includes a forceful point of view, Viégas and Wattenberg also point out that they are still “visualizations of data done by artists with the intent of making art” (Viégas and Wattenberg, 2007: 2; emphasis added). Thus, it is crucial that data-driven artworks forgo artistic beauty as their motive, and instead use an actual dataset to emphasize the creator’s artistic intent.

With that, we can then start looking at the data-driven artwork I created in relation to Viégas and Wattenberg’s definition. While I considered various ideas for the artwork, such as creating glitch art of different company logos that represents the amount of data they have ‘lost’, I felt this did not challenge the meaning of data visualizations enough. As such, I decided to challenge myself further, landing on the idea of creating an interactive countdown clock/timer to indicate when the user is most likely to have their data breached next – using my API to find the average time between data breaches linked to the user’s account. This was done with the intent of making the information more personal and relatable to the user, making them more aware of the impact and importance of this topic.

However, some might argue that this cannot be considered a data-driven artwork, as it only makes use of text – but I would disagree, as this was deliberate. Whilst most traditional data visualizations prioritize the use of visual, over text-based, representation; I purposefully subverted this expectation to aid my artworks point-of-view. By using a timer as the center point of the visualization, it is clear what information must be focused on; and with the countdown clock/timer always ticking forward (with audio clock ticks), users are also made aware of how frequently their personal data is being shared on the internet unwillingly - similair to a doomsday clock.

In conclusion, we can then see that although my data-driven artwork may seem simple, every choice was deliberate. By reflecting on the artwork, we can see that it clearly meets Viégas and Wattenberg’s definition of a data-driven artwork – as it uses my data with the intent of making threat of data breaches more personal. Not only that, but it commits various sins of traditional scientific data visualizations to create a forceful point-of-view. Although, it is important to note that some users will have passed the predicted date for a their next breach, or have none at all, and as such I have also accounted for these factors – which can be tested with the following emails: foo@bar.com (predicted date yet to arrive), multiple-breaches@hibp-integration-tests.com (predicted date passed), opt-out-breach@hibp-integration-tests.com (no breaches).
