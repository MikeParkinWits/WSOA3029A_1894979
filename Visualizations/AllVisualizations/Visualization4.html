Reflection

12 November 2021 | Words: 543 | Data Visualization

While not every data visualization needs to incorporate interactivity to meaningfully portray their message, when utilized correctly it can greatly enhance the overall narrative of the visualization. As such, in this blog post I aim to critically reflect on my third data visualization – exploring my implementation of interactivity within it. To do this, I start by explaining both the narrative I am trying to portray, and the visualization type chosen. Thereafter, I explore how I chose to incorporate interactivity into my visualization, evaluating its success in relation to the seven components of interactivity for visualization that Evanthia Dimara and Charles Perin highlight in their 2020 text What is Interaction for Data Visualization? (Dimara and Perin, 2020).

However, before we can look at how I incorporated interactivity into my data visualization, we must understand the visualization itself – beginning with the narrative that I was trying to portray. While the data visualizations I have created thus far have been fairly simple, allowing me to grow my skillset, for my third data visualization I wanted to challenge myself. As such, I landed on the idea of recreating one of my previous data visualizations in a new, and interesting, way. From this, I then deciding to rethink my first data visualization, concluding that a bubble graph would allow me to explore not only the biggest data breaches of all time, but also the size of all data breaches in relation to one another. Although, I soon realized that this idea would not make full use of the bubble graph, as I only planned on comparing the size of bubbles and had no intention of utilizing different colours. As such, I decided that by combining my previous two narratives, I would then be able to create an even more interesting narrative – the increasing size of data breaches in recent years, where different coloured bubbles represent data breaches before and after 2015 (the median date for my data).

While I believe that this alone does sufficiently communicate my narrative, I knew that the inclusion of interactivity could greatly enhance it – if incorporated well. As such, to better understand how to meaningfully incorporate interactivity, I looked to Dimara and Perin, who define interaction for visualization as “interaction for visualization is the interplay between a person and a data interface involving a data-related intent, at least one action from the person and an interface reaction that is perceived as such” (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 8; emphasis added). Using this, I then concluded that to create meaningful interactions, interactivity must meet the end-user’s intent in a timely, and accurate, manner. As such, I determined that the most likely intent for the user was learning more about each data breach. This then resulted in the idea of utilizing the action of hovering over a bubble to provide the end-user with more information about each data breach.

In brief, I then believe that, although not necessary, the inclusion of interactivity helped to greatly enhance the message for this data visualization – ultimately allowing the end-user to explore additional information that was previously inaccessible. Not only that, but by consulting Dimara and Perin’s definition for interaction in visualizations, before incorporating interaction, I believe that my inclusion of interactivity was as successful as possible – allowing the user to quickly, and easily, meet their data-related intent when requested.

References
