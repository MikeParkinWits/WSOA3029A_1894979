The Data Lost to Data Breaches Each Year

Reflection

28 September 2021 | Words: 452 | Data Visualization

While data visualizations may seem simple and intuitive to the reader, if they are not well thought out, they can be extremely challenging to read and understand. Thus, in this blog post I critically reflect on my second data visualization – looking at the considerations made to ensure that it was as readable as possible.

When reflecting on any data visualization, we must first understand what information was trying to be portrayed, and the type of visualization used as a result. In my first visualization, I compared the ten biggest digital data breaches in history, with the goal of informing the reader of the size differences between these different data breaches and just how large they were. For this visualization, I wanted to take this a step further by showing the total size of data breaches year-over-year. Thus, my goal was to give the reader an indication of the general trend of data breaches, alluding to they are becoming more common and require the reader to be more cautious with where they share their data. As such, like my first visualization, I decided to utilize a chart – as I was still comparing the size of data, and as Qi Li states in her 2020 book Embodying Data, charts are best used to portray information about size (Li, 2020: 29 - 30). However, unlike my first data visualization I chose to make use of a line chart, as I was more concerned with showing the change over a set period, as opposed to the exact numbers.

With that, we can then start to look at the specific considerations that I made when creating my second data visualization. Like my first, I wanted to keep this visualization simple as I was still experimenting with the D3.js library – although, simplicity does not mean easy to create. As is the case with all my visualizations thus far, I have made a conscious effort to keep them as minimalist as possible to reduce cognitive load – removing any unnecessary elements. However, a specific consideration that I had to make with this visualization was about the inclusion of gridlines and data markers. I decided that because the goal of my visualization was to show the general trend of data breaches over time, the inclusion of grid lines and markers would imply that each specific data point should be read, and as such I removed them both.

In conclusion, in this blog post I looked at the reasons behind my chosen data visualization type, and the considerations behind its creation. Not only that, but I also highlighted the similarity in thought process behind all my data visualizations thus far, with both data visualizations adding to my website’s consistency overall and minimalism.
