Top Stolen Information in Data Breaches

Reflection

07 December 2021 | Words: 499 | Data Visualization

Although I believe that through the creation of various data visualizations, and data-driven artworks, I have been able to teach users about the dangers of oversharing their information online; there is still one area I believe that I have not to cover. As such, in this blog post I aim to reflect on this new visualization. To do this, I start by outlining the reasoning, and narrative, behind my choices – before looking more closely at the considerations made during the visualization’s creation. Thereafter, I conclude by reflecting on whether I believe that I was successful in achieving my intended narrative.

First, we must start by understanding the new dataset that I chose to visualize. Up until now, the main intent of all my visualizations were to inform users about the increasing threat of data breaches – with visualizations around data breach sizes, and the looming threat this poses to the user. Although, while I had been preaching for users to try and reduce their risk of having their data stolen, I realized I had not actually told users what data they should be keeping safe. As such, for this visualization I decided to visualize the most stolen information during a data breach – with the aim of making users question what personal information they share online.

To do this, I then decided to use a pie chart for my visualization – as it is an informative way of comparing proportional information. Although, it is important to acknowledge that pie charts have garnered a bad reputation within the visualization community, with scholars such as Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic who, in their 2015 book Storytelling with data: a data visualization guide for business professionals, disown the visualization method, and its usefulness, all together – stating that they are deceptive and misleading (Knaflic, 2015). However, I believe that this can be said for any visualization when used incorrectly/maliciously (ibid.). Thus, I deem the pie chart useful and serving a specific purpose, when utilized correctly, well – comparing proportional information together easily.

However, in order to decrease the confusion that Knaflic point out, I also make use of interactivity. When the user hovers over any part of the chart, a bubble appears indicating the percentage that that section holds. This then allows numbers to be hidden from the visualization for the most part, which aids in its visual communication, but still allows those curious to receive this information. Although, even with this I would say that I did not reach my potential the graphs potential in terms of interactivity. As a result of unforeseen circumstances, and time constraints, I was unable to implement all interactivity planned, with options to switch between different time periods, to see the change in stolen information, scrapped.

In conclusion, we can then see that although I was unable to meet my goal for interactivity in this chart, I would still argue that I was able to successfully portray the topmost stolen information in data breaches – helping to keep users more informed and safer. online.

References
